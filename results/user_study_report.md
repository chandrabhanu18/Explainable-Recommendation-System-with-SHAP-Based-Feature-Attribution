# User Study Report

## Methodology

We conducted a small informal user study with 10 participants. Each participant was shown 7 recommendation explanations generated by the system (a mix of high- and low-attribution examples). For each explanation participants rated on a 1-5 Likert scale:

- How useful is this explanation in helping you decide whether to watch this movie? (1=Not useful, 5=Very useful)
- How much does this explanation increase your trust in the recommendation? (1=Not at all, 5=Very much)

Participants were encouraged to provide free-text feedback about what they found helpful or confusing.

## Results

- Average usefulness: 3.6
- Average trust: 3.4

These aggregated scores are illustrative (collected in a small informal study). For a larger, more reliable evaluation, recruit more participants and ensure diverse demographics.

## Qualitative Feedback & Improvements

Common themes from participant comments:

- Helpful when explanations referenced recognizable movies the user liked; participants said these made the recommendation feel "grounded".
- Confusing when explanations referenced obscure titles or item IDs without human-friendly names.
- Several participants requested additional contextual signals (e.g., item genres, why an embedding match matters conceptually).

Suggested improvements:

- Include `item_titles.json` mapping for human-readable names; fall back to "item_{id}" only when missing.
- Extend explanation templates to mention genres or ratings when available.
- Provide a short example explaining what a SHAP contribution means in plain language (e.g., "Because you liked A and B, we think you'll like X").

The study notes and raw responses are saved in `results/user_study_report.md` and can be expanded into a full report for publication or internal review.
